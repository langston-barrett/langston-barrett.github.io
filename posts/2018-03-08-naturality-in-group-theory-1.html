Naturality in group theory

Part 1: intertwiners

This is part of a series of posts exploring concepts in group theory that can be examined fruitfully using natural transformations (which will be covered in the last post in the series). It will be most accessible and useful to readers with some familiarity with group theory and linear algebra.

Review

Definition. A group G consists of:

  • a set also denoted G,
  • a binary operation ⋅ : G × G → G called multiplication,
  • a unary operation ( − )−1 : G → G called inversion,
  • and an element u ∈ G called the identity.

These data are subject to the following axioms:

  1. Associativity: (g1 ⋅ g2)⋅g3 = g1 ⋅ (g2 ⋅ g3) for all g1, g2, g3 ∈ G.
  2. Unity: u ⋅ g = g ⋅ u = g for all g ∈ G.
  3. Inverses: g ⋅ g−1 = g−1 ⋅ g = u.

The operation is often abbreviated by juxtaposition, e.g. g ⋅ g′=gg′.

Definition. Given groups G and N, a function f : G → N is a group homomorphism if for all g, g′∈G, f(gg′) = f(g)f(g′). A homomorphism is an isomorphism if it has an inverse.

Automorphism groups

Automorphism groups are an important class of examples. They have the self-isomorphisms (automorphisms) of some algebraic object as the elements and composition as the operation (the identity function serves as a unit).

The simplest example is the symmetric group 𝔖X on a set X has bijections X → X as elements. All other automorphism groups are subgroups of this one; composed of functions that respect some sort of structure on X.

The automorphism group of a group G is the set of group isomorphisms G → G. For every g ∈ G, (left) conjugation by g, the function given by x ↦ gxg−1 is an isomorphism of g. Automorphisms that arise from conjugation are called inner automorphisms. The composition of two inner automorphisms is again inner, so they form a (normal) subgroup of the whole automorphism group.

The automorphism group or general linear group of a vector space V over a field F consists of invertible linear transformations V → V. The concrete realization of this group is the matrix group over F, the group of invertible n × n matrices with entries in the with matrix multiplication as the operation. The automorphism group of a field, considered as a 1-dimensional vector space over itself, is just the group of units (invertible elements under multiplication).

In general, the automorphism group of an object O is denoted Aut(O).

Group actions

Automorphism groups are used to define the general and pervasive notion of a group “acting” on an object.

Definition. Let X be a set and G a group. An action of G on X is a group homomorphism ρ : G → 𝔖X. A G-set is a set equipped with an action by G.

Equivalently, a group action is an function ρ : G × X → X that obeys the following axioms:

  1. Identity: for all x ∈ X, ρ(u, x)=x
  2. Composition: for all x ∈ X and g, g′∈G, ρ(g, ρ(g′,x)) = f(gg′,x)

Actions are also called permutation representations. It is often convenient to curry the first argument, writing ρ(g) for the function x ↦ ρ(g, x).

Every group acts on itself

  • by left multiplication x ↦ gx,
  • by right multiplication x ↦ xg,
  • by left conjugation x ↦ gxg−1,
  • by right conjugation x ↦ g−1xg, and
  • trivially, x ↦ x.

(In fact, this trivial action works for any group and any underlying set).

When are two G-sets “the same”? What is a good definition of isomorphism between them? Consider two actions of the two-element group G ≔ ℤ/2ℤ = {0, 1} on a two element set X ≔ {a, b}. Since we know ρ(0)=idX by definition of an action, it remains to define ρ(1). There are only two possibilities:

  • a ↦ a and b ↦ b, or
  • a ↦ b and b ↦ a.

These two representations shouldn’t be isomorphic. If an isomorphism of G-sets were merely a bijective function of the underlying sets, then the identity function on X would be an isomorphism between them. We need another condition:

Definition. An isomorphism between two G-sets (X, ρ) and (Y, σ) is a bijective function f : X → Y that intertwines the action of G, that is,

f ∘ ρ(g)=σ(g)∘f

for any g ∈ G. In other words, the following diagram commutes:

This definition works in the sense that there is no isomorphism between the actions considered above. It also feels right: when we consider ρ(g) and σ(g) as unary operations on X and Y, intertwining them is like obeying the rule f(g−1)=f(g)−1 that applies to normal group homomorphisms.

Linear representations

Many group actions come with a spatial flavor.

  • The symmetric group 𝔖n acts on n-dimensional Euclidean space by permuting the axes (more generally, only any n-fold product space by permuting the coordinates).
  • The group of units of a field F acts on any F-vector space by dilation.
  • The group of symmetries of a square acts on the plane by extending the rotation to include the whole space.
  • The group of automorphisms of a vector or topological space acts by application.

For the remainder of this section, we’ll focus on the actions of groups on vector spaces. To this end, fix a field F.

Definition. Let V be a vector space over F, and G a group. A (linear) representation of G on V is a group homomorphism ρ : G → Aut(V).

If ρ : G → Aut(V) is a group representation, the following equalities hold:

  1. ρ(g)(v + w)=ρ(g)(v)+ρ(g)(w),
  2. ρ(g)(c · v)=c · ρ(g)(v),

for all g ∈ G, v, w ∈ V, and c ∈ F by virtue of ρ(g) being a linear transformation, and

  1. ρ(g)(ρ(g′)(v)) = ρ(gg′)(v),

for all g, g′∈G and v ∈ V since ρ is a group homomorphism.

Let (X, ρ) be a finite G-set, let n ≔ |X|, and let V be a n-dimensional vector space over F. Choose a basis {bx}x ∈ X for V, indexed by the elements in X. The regular representation of G on V is the one that permutes the basis elements according to ρ, that is bx ↦ bρ(g, x).
